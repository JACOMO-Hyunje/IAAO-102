## Section II — Basis of Income (Leases & Rent Types)

**Q: Define a real estate lease.**
A: A written document transferring the rights to use/occupy land or structures from owner to another for a specified period in return for specified rent.

**Q: When analyzing a lease, what 3 things should you evaluate?**
A: Quantity (amount of rent), quality (the income stream), durability (length of lease).

**Q: A lease is both a ___ and a ___.**
A: A contract (tenant's obligation to pay rent) and a conveyance (landlord grants right to occupy).

**Q: Leased fee interest vs. leasehold interest — who holds which?**
A: Landlord holds the leased fee interest (value of the leases to the owner). Tenant holds the leasehold interest (right of use/occupancy under the lease).

**Q: Positive leasehold value vs. negative leasehold value — which party benefits?**
A: If contract rent < market rent → positive leasehold value (benefits tenant). If market rent drops below contract rent → negative leasehold value (benefits landlord).

**Q: Define the 5 types of rent covered in this chapter.**
A:
- **Market (economic) rent** — what a property should command in the open market; typically used to calculate income in the income approach.
- **Contract rent** — the rent actually being paid per the current lease.
- **Excess rent** — contract rent > market rent.
- **Deficit rent (leasehold rent/income)** — market rent > contract rent (tenant benefits from the difference).
- **Effective rent** — lease base rent minus rent concessions; used as a common denominator to compare leases with different provisions.

**Q: Effective rent formula, and why do we use it?**
A: Effective rent = Base rent − Rent concessions. Used to compare leases with different terms on an apples-to-apples basis, and to help develop market rent.

**Q: List 4 reasons contract rent may not reflect market rent.**
A: (1) Built-to-suit improvements priced to cost, not market; (2) technological obsolescence; (3) competition-driven obsolescence; (4) unreasonably high land cost from site-specific business needs.

**Q: Lease term categories — month-to-month, short-term, long-term, renewal — how are they distinguished?**
A: Month-to-month = no long-term security, may be unwritten. Short-term = generally <5 years, written, may have renewal clause (office/retail/apartment common). Long-term = >5 years, always written (industrial/big-box/fast-food common). Renewal = built-in extension option in the original lease.

**Q: Graduated lease = ?**
A: A.k.a. step-up/step-down lease — rent changes at one or more specified points during the lease term.

**Q: In a percentage lease, define minimum base rent and overage rent.**
A: Minimum base rent = the fixed portion. Overage rent = the variable portion above the minimum, typically based on % of tenant's sales/revenue.

## Section II continued — Expense Responsibility / Lease Types by Who Pays What

**Q: Gross lease vs. net lease — the general rule of thumb?**
A: Gross lease = landlord pays operating expenses. Net lease = landlord passes operating expenses to tenant. (Caution: terminology varies by market — always confirm actual expense responsibilities rather than relying on the label.)

**Q: Rank these from most landlord-expense-burden to most tenant-expense-burden: Full-service, Modified gross, Single net, Double net (NN), Triple net (NNN), Absolute net.**
A: Full-service (landlord pays everything, incl. real estate taxes) → Gross → Modified gross (shared) → Single net → Double net (NN) → Triple net (NNN) → Absolute net (tenant pays everything, landlord pays nothing).

**Q: What specifically does "triple net" mean the tenant reimburses?**
A: Insurance, real estate taxes, and CAM (common area maintenance) — separate from base rent — plus their own utilities.

**Q: What's the difference between full-service lease and gross industrial lease?**
A: Full-service = predominantly multi-tenant office; landlord pays base rent-included expenses including RE taxes. Gross industrial = landlord pays insurance/taxes/structural maintenance; tenant pays utilities and property maintenance.

**Q: Expense stop vs. expense cap — who bears costs above/below the threshold?**
A: Expense stop: landlord pays operating expenses up to a set level; tenant reimburses anything above it. Expense cap: tenant bears expenses up to a set level; landlord picks up anything above it. (These are opposite directions of responsibility — a common trap.)

**Q: Tenant improvement (TI) allowance — what happens if landlord pays and wants to recover the cost through rent?**
A: Landlord divides total TI cost by lease term (years or payments) to get an annualized recovery amount, then converts to a per-square-foot add-on to base rent.

## Section IV — Investment Class

**Q: What 3 categories of characteristics define an investment class?**
A: Physical features (effective age, condition, quality, amenities), Location (access, visibility), Economic characteristics (rent levels, occupancy, management).

**Q: Distinguish Class A, B, and C properties.**
A:
- **Class A**: effective age <10 yrs, well-maintained, contemporary design, above-average materials, strong access/visibility — commands highest rents, attracts credit tenants.
- **Class B**: effective age 10–30 yrs, average condition/materials, may need short-lived item replacement; often value-added investment targets.
- **Class C**: effective age >30 yrs (or effective age ≥ actual age), fair/poor condition, deferred maintenance/functional obsolescence common — lowest rents, longest lease-up, redevelopment targets.

**Q: Key caveat about investment class ratings?**
A: No formal international standard exists; classification is relative to the specific submarket and property use — a Class A building in one submarket may not be Class A in another.

## Section V — Rental Units of Comparison

**Q: Match the unit of comparison to the property type: rent per unit, rent per room, rent per space, rent per square foot.**
A:
- Rent per unit → apartments, self-storage, condos
- Rent per room → apartments, motels, hotels
- Rent per space → mobile home parks, travel trailer parks, parking garages
- Rent per square foot → shopping centers, retail, office, warehouse (annual basis); apartments (monthly basis)

**Q: GLA vs. NLA — define both, and what's the difference between them called?**
A: GLA (gross leasable area) = entire building area. NLA (net leasable area) = only the floor area occupied by the tenant. The difference = common space.

**Q: Why does it matter whether GLA or NLA is used in a rent-per-square-foot comparison?**
A: Mixing them creates a substantial value error — you must know which basis a comparable's rent uses and keep it consistent throughout the analysis (can't apply an NLA-based rate to a GLA-based area).

**Q: How do you decide which unit of comparison (e.g., rent/unit vs. rent/SF) is most appropriate for a data set?**
A: Calculate the percentage spread [(High − Low) ÷ Low] for each unit of comparison across your comparables — the one with the **lowest** percentage spread is preferred (most consistency/least noise).

**Q: GIM definition and formula.**
A: Gross Income Multiplier = the relationship between gross income and property value (sale price). GIM = Sale Price ÷ Annual Gross Income. Used with commercial/apartment properties; can be based on PGI or EGI (if EGI-based, called EGIM).

## Section VI — Income and Expense Statement (35% of chapter — the core math)

**Q: Draw/recall the income and expense statement flow (top to bottom).**
A:
```
Potential Gross Income (PGI)
 − Vacancy & Collection Loss
 + Miscellaneous Income
 = Effective Gross Income (EGI)
 − Operating Expenses (incl. reserves for replacement)
 = Net Operating Income (NOI)
```

**Q: Define Potential Gross Income (PGI).**
A: The annual economic (market) rent for the property at 100% occupancy, from all scheduled sources — reflects market rent, not necessarily contract rent.

**Q: Two vacancy rate formulas.**
A: Vacant units ÷ Total units = Vacancy Rate, OR Vacant square footage ÷ Total square footage = Vacancy Rate.

**Q: Collection loss formula (two steps).**
A: Billable rent − Collected rent = Uncollected rent. Uncollected rent ÷ Billable rent = Collection loss rate. (Vacancy and collection loss are typically combined into one % of PGI in the reconstructed statement.)

**Q: EGI formula.**
A: EGI = PGI − (Vacancy + Collection Loss) + Appropriate Miscellaneous Income.

**Q: Give 5+ examples of miscellaneous income.**
A: Parking fees, vending machines, coin laundry, application fees, pet rent, late fees, forfeited deposits, cable/phone/data plans.

**Q: Fixed vs. variable expenses vs. reserves for replacement — define each.**
A: Fixed = doesn't vary with occupancy rate. Variable = varies with occupancy rate. Reserves for replacement = annual charges for short-lived items that must be replaced before the improvement's useful life ends.

**Q: Operating Expense Ratio (OER) formula.**
A: OER = Total Expenses ÷ Effective Gross Income.

**Q: Reserves for replacement — walk through the 4-step calculation.**
A: (1) Estimate useful life of the item, (2) estimate replacement cost new, (3) 100% ÷ useful life = annual reserve %, (4) Replacement cost new × annual reserve % = annual reserve charge.

**Q: List the "proper" operating expense categories (7).**
A: Management, Insurance, Utilities, Maintenance, Employee wages/benefits, Grounds keeping, Miscellaneous expenses (+ Reserves for replacement).

**Q: List the "improper" expenses that must be EXCLUDED from NOI, and why each is excluded.**
A:
- **Depreciation** — handled via recapture in the cap rate instead; tax depreciation ≠ appraisal recapture.
- **Debt service (P&I)** — a financing cost of a specific owner's capital structure, reflected in the discount/cap rate, not NOI; appraisals assume free-and-clear ownership.
- **Owner's personal expenses (e.g., personal income tax)** — not necessary for generating property income.
- **Capital improvements** — not annual expenses; they increase value/life/income instead (e.g., adding a pool).
- **Franchise fees / special corporation costs** — charges against the owner/business, not the real estate itself (exception: can be proper for hotel appraisals when comps are also franchised).

**Q: Real estate taxes — proper or improper expense? (This is a conditional one — a favorite trick question.)**
A: **It depends on appraisal purpose.** Proper expense for non-ad-valorem appraisals. **Improper/excluded** for ad valorem (property tax assessment) appraisals — because you can't deduct taxes you're trying to determine (circular logic); instead, taxes are captured via the effective tax rate component of the cap rate.

**Q: Given the reconstruction demonstrations, what's the general adjustment technique when an owner's statement covers less than 12 months (e.g., 8 months)?**
A: Prorate: (amount ÷ months reported) × 12 to annualize.

**Q: Why must expense reimbursements (e.g., CAM charges billed to tenants under a net lease) be included in PGI, not just netted out of expenses?**
A: Reimbursements are subject to the same vacancy/collection loss risk as rent — if the unit is vacant, the landlord doesn't receive the reimbursement either. They belong in income, and the landlord's own actual paid expense (before reimbursement) appears in the expense section.

## ⚠️ Common Mix-Up Traps

1. **Excess rent vs. Deficit/leasehold rent** — excess = contract > market (favors landlord); deficit/leasehold = market > contract (favors tenant, since they're underpaying relative to market).
2. **Expense stop vs. expense cap** — direction of who pays above the threshold is reversed between the two; read carefully.
3. **Real estate taxes as an expense** — the *only* conditional item in the proper/improper list; the correct answer depends entirely on whether the appraisal is for ad valorem tax purposes.
4. **GLA vs. NLA** — always confirm which basis a given rent figure is quoted on before comparing or multiplying.
5. **Vacancy & collection loss are two separate formulas** but are usually **combined into a single %** in the reconstructed statement — don't present them as one calculation when asked to define them separately.
6. **"Proper" expense categories vs. Chapter 1 mortgage/financing lists** — don't confuse "reserves for replacement" (Ch. 2, short-lived items) with "recapture rate" (Ch. 1, wasting asset return via cap rate) — they're related concepts in different places in the formula (expense side vs. rate side).
