## Study Guide & Flashcards

## Section I — Intro to Income Capitalization

**Q: What is the income capitalization approach, and what is it one of three of?**
A: One of the three traditional approaches to value (income, cost, sales comparison — standard appraisal framework; this SRM chapter doesn't name the other two explicitly, but assumes familiarity with the three-approaches concept). In this approach, value is estimated by converting anticipated benefits (income/rent) arising from ownership of an income-producing property — this conversion is also called the capitalization process.

**Q: What are the 3 components of the capitalization process?**
A: Value, Net Operating Income, Capitalization Rate.

**Q: Name the 8 economic principles underlying income capitalization.**
A: Anticipation, Change, Competition, Substitution, Balance, Contribution, Supply, Demand.

**Q: Which principle is described as "the underlying principle which provides the basis of the income capitalization approach"?**
A: Anticipation — value is created by expectation of future benefits.

**Q: Competition among sellers vs. competition among buyers — what does each cause?**
A: Sellers competing → oversupply (prices/profits fall). Buyers competing → shortage (prices/profits rise).

**Q: Which principle says prices/rents/rates-of-return are set by equally desirable substitutes, and provides the basis for selecting the cap/discount rate?**
A: Substitution.

**Q: Market value — give the 5 conditions implicit in the definition.**
A: (1) Buyer & seller typically motivated, (2) both well-informed/advised acting in own best interest, (3) reasonable market exposure time, (4) cash or equivalent financing terms, (5) price unaffected by special financing/concessions.

**Q: Market value vs. investment value — what's the difference?**
A: Market value = most probable price in a competitive open market. Investment value = worth to a *specific* investor; may or may not match market value.

**Q: What are the 3 components of real property rights?**
A: Ownership entity (individual/corporate/partnership), Financial interest (equity/debt), Legal estate (fee simple, leased fee, leasehold).

## Section II — Real Estate Investment

**Q: What 5 questions does an investor ask when analyzing opportunities?**
A: (1) How much will it cost? (2) How much will I get back? (3) When will I get it back? (4) What are the risks? (5) What is the return of a real estate investment compared to other investments with similar risks? *(This is a different list from the "9 factors influencing investor decisions" below — don't merge them.)*

**Q: Two categories of investment, per this chapter?**
A: Fixed-dollar investments (low risk/gain, e.g. savings account) and Growth investments (higher risk/opportunity, e.g. stocks, real estate).

**Q: What two things does every investor want, regardless of type?**
A: Return *of* investment (recapture rate) and return *on* investment (overall yield/discount rate).

**Q: List the 9 factors influencing investor decisions (savings account vs. real estate comparison).**
A: Safety, Liquidity, Size, Collateral, Time, Management, Appreciation, Income tax, Leverage.

**Q: Define leverage, and its 3 variants.**
A: Leverage = borrowing funds hoping to earn a greater return than their cost.
- Positive leverage: property return > cost of borrowed funds
- Negative leverage: cost of borrowed funds > property return
- Neutral leverage: cost of borrowed funds = property return

**Q: What is a "value-added investment"?**
A: Buying a property, improving it, and selling at an opportune time for gain — can shorten the typical real estate holding period.

## Section III — Real Estate Financing (34% of chapter — biggest topic)

**Q: List the 4 most common methods of financing real estate.**
A: Cash, Trust deed, Land contract (contract for deed), Mortgage.

**Q: What is a trust deed, and why is it used?**
A: Like a mortgage — transfers title to a trustee for the lender's benefit; borrower keeps use/occupancy. Used to avoid a foreclosure proceeding on default.

**Q: What is a land contract, a.k.a.?**
A: Contract for deed / installment sales contract — small down payment, balance paid over the contract term.

**Q: List the 7 types of mortgages by purpose/structure (not repayment).**
A: First (conventional), Junior, Purchase-money, Construction loan, Open-end, Chattel, Package.

**Q: Junior mortgage = ?**
A: Second mortgage — recorded after a prior lien; typically 5–50% of property value.

**Q: Chattel mortgage vs. package mortgage?**
A: Chattel = personal property only. Package = real estate + personal property together (e.g., apartment building with stoves/dishwashers/fridges included).

**Q: List the 6 sources of mortgage loans.**
A: Commercial banks, Credit unions, Mortgage companies, Life insurance companies, Conduit lenders, Private money lenders.

**Q: What's distinctive about credit unions as a mortgage source?**
A: Typically allow prepayment without penalty + flexible refinancing.

**Q: What do conduit lenders do?**
A: Originate commercial mortgages, hold briefly, then securitize into CMBS (Commercial Mortgage-Backed Securities) sold to investors.

**Q: List the 5 mortgage types classified by *repayment structure*.**
A: Amortized (fixed-rate), Variable/adjustable-rate (ARM), Straight, Partially amortized (balloon), Reverse.

**Q: What is a balloon payment?**
A: The lump-sum payment of remaining principal due at the end of a partially amortized mortgage's term.

**Q: How does a reverse mortgage work?**
A: Low-interest loan (typically senior homeowners) using home equity as collateral; loan amount is a % of value based on the *youngest* homeowner's age; repaid when the last surviving homeowner moves out or passes away.

**Q: List the 4 variables affecting mortgage payments (Demonstrations 1-1 to 1-4).**
A: Amount borrowed, Interest rate, Term of loan, Frequency of payments.

**Q: Directionally, what happens to monthly payment and total interest paid as each variable increases?**
A:
- ↑ Amount borrowed → ↑ monthly payment, ↑ total interest
- ↑ Interest rate → ↑ monthly payment, ↑ total interest
- ↑ Payment frequency → ↓ payment size, ↓ total interest
- ↑ Term length → ↓ monthly payment, but ↑ total interest paid

## Section IV — Primary Rates

**Q: What are the 4 components of the overall yield rate (YO / discount rate) under the summation concept?**
A: Safe rate, Risk rate, Rate for non-liquidity, Rate for management.

**Q: Define each of the 4 summation components briefly.**
A:
- Safe rate: base rate on safest investments (e.g., govt-insured)
- Risk rate: added compensation for investment risk
- Non-liquidity rate: added compensation for time to convert to cash
- Management rate: added compensation for decision-making/management burden

**Q: What does the recapture rate provide for?**
A: Return *of* the investment in the wasting (depreciating) portion of the asset — similar to a depreciation rate.

**Q: What does the effective tax rate (ETR) reflect?**
A: The relationship between real estate taxes and the value of the property.

## Section V — Generic Capitalization Formulas

**Q: State the IRV formula and its 3 rearrangements.**
A:
- I (income) = Rate × Value
- R (rate) = Income ÷ Value
- V (value) = Income ÷ Rate
- "I" = Net Operating Income (NOI)

**Q: State the VIF formula and its 3 rearrangements.**
A:
- V (value) = Income × Factor
- I (income) = Value ÷ Factor
- F (factor) = Value ÷ Income
- "I" = Gross Income (not NOI — key distinction from IRV!)

**Q: VIF's factor is also known as...?**
A: Gross Income Multiplier (GIM) for commercial property (Sale Price ÷ Annual Gross Income); Gross Rent Multiplier for residential.

**Q: Worked example (Demonstration 1-5/1-6) — what's the key mechanical difference to watch between IRV and VIF when applied to the same property?**
A: IRV uses *net* operating income (income minus expenses) against a rate; VIF uses *gross* income against a multiplier/factor. Same property, different income base.

## ⚠️ Common Mix-Up Traps (based on how review questions are phrased)

1. **Anticipation vs. Substitution** — both get quizzed as fill-in-the-blank; anticipation = future benefits expectation, substitution = price-setting via equivalent alternatives.
2. **Three similar-sounding lists** — the "5 questions investors ask" (Section II), "9 factors influencing investor decisions" (Section II), and "4 summation concept parts" (Section IV) are three *different* lists. Easy to blend them under time pressure.
3. **Mortgage classified by purpose** (first, junior, purchase-money, construction, open-end, chattel, package) **vs. classified by repayment structure** (amortized, ARM, straight, partially amortized, reverse) — the review questions ask about repayment-structure types specifically (Q12), so don't answer with the purpose-based list.
4. **IRV's "I" = NOI, but VIF's "I" = Gross Income** — the single most common formula mix-up.
5. Recapture rate (return *of* investment) vs. overall yield/discount rate (return *on* investment) — easy to swap under time pressure.
